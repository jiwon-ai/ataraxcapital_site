# Why I killed a strategy that kept making me lose my calm

It was one of the first strategies I built, a statistical-arbitrage spread between two index futures.

At first, it looked promising. Not spectacular, not dramatic, but quietly attractive in exactly the dangerous way: small profits, again and again. It gave me the feeling that the system could make money almost every day. That was the part I wanted to believe in.

The problem was not that I forgot about commissions. I knew trading had costs. The real problem was more subtle, and more personal.

The strategy made money in a way that trained the wrong part of me.

It would collect small gains steadily, and then, once in a while, it would face a move large enough to test my composure. That was where the damage happened. Not in the ordinary losses. A system can be designed for ordinary losses. The damage happened when one bad sequence made me want to intervene: widen the stop, re-enter too quickly, recover the loss, increase size, or convince myself that this time was different.

That is not a small flaw. That is the whole problem.

A strategy that makes a little money for many days and then invites one large emotional mistake is not safe just because the backtest looks profitable. It is dangerous because it gives the illusion of control before it demands control at the exact moment when control is hardest.

I killed it for that reason.

Not because it was stupid. Not because it had no logic. It had logic. It had structure. It had moments where it worked. But it required a version of me that I cannot always guarantee in live trading. And a system that depends on my perfect emotional state is not a system. It is discretion pretending to be automation.

This was an important lesson for Atarax.

The purpose of the system is not to find something that wins every day. That desire is already a warning sign. The purpose is to build something that can survive the days when I am tired, impatient, frustrated, or tempted to force the market to give back what it took.

A strategy is not only tested by its average return. It is tested by the behavior it creates in the person running it.

Does it make me overtrade?
Does it make me watch every candle?
Does it make me believe I am safe because I have been winning in small amounts?
Does it create a situation where one emotional decision can erase many disciplined ones?

If the answer is yes, then the strategy does not belong near capital.

That was why I turned it off. It was not a failure of intelligence. It was a failure of compatibility. The strategy may have looked profitable, but it did not protect the calm I am trying to build. And for Atarax, that matters as much as the numbers.

I do not want a system that rewards me for weeks and then waits for one weak moment to take everything back. I want a system whose risk is clear before the trade, whose loss is acceptable before it happens, and whose rules do not require me to become superhuman in order to follow them.

> Most of the work is not finding what can make money.
> Most of the work is removing what can make me lose myself.
